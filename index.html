Prices in North Carolina

Over the past several years, North Carolinians have seen an increase in prices across many spending categories. Of course, such increases are not unique to North Carolina, or even to the United States – the impact of the COVID-19 Pandemic has been felt economically across the world. This web app tells the story of prices and their political impact in North Carolina since 2020.

2020

North Carolinians started 2020 with expectations of continued improvement in economic conditions. Consumer sentiment was relatively high, employment was stable, and talk of inflation or rising prices was scarce. When the COVID-19 Pandemic hit, this rosy picture was shaken. While prices didn’t immediately rise (and indeed fell in some categories, such as gas), the pandemic registered as an immediate economic threat. In response to widespread increases in unemployment and other severe economic impacts, Congress passed significant stimulus that included direct cash benefits – stimulus that very successfully curbed the economic fallout in comparison to other countries. However, this infusion of money, along with pandemic-induced shocks to supply chains, caused inflationary pressures that played out over the years to come.

2022

By 2022, the word “inflation” was on everyone’s lips. Compared to zero headlines mentioning “inflation” in 2020, the Raleigh News & Observer ran 60 articles in 2022 that included the word “inflation” in the headline. This increase in attention was reasonable: prices had shot up, in North Carolina and across the country, and rates of inflation were high. Beyond being a concern for people’s pocketbooks, inflation had also emerged as a hot political issue just in time for the midterm elections. Congress passed a cornerstone of Joe Biden’s legislative platform: the Inflation Reduction Act. Drug pricing reform, investment in American clean energy initiatives, and increased taxes on corporations and the wealthy were key provisions in the IRA. Consumer discontent, however, continued to rise along with prices.

2024

While inflation began to slow as Biden neared the end of his term, economic sentiment remained poor. Inflation remained a charged topic in advance of 2024 election. Republicans hammered Biden on prices and Donald Trump made sweeping promises to lower prices, while Kamala Harris rolled out policy proposals aimed at alleviating costs. Despite lowering rates of inflation and overall strong growth in real wages (and therefore purchasing power), voters expressed strongly negative views about the economy. Donald Trump’s reelection in November 2024 was in line with a worldwide anti-incumbent backlash, which many experts attributed to inflation.

2025

Though discontent over inflation helped propel Donald Trump to a second term, voters are already realizing that his promise to "bring prices down starting on Day One” was all talk. Instead, Trump has pursued tariff policies likely to dramatically increase prices across a range of consumer categories. North Carolina families regularly buy products that come from Mexico, Canada, China, and Europe – products that will now be hit with tariffs ranging from 10% to over 100%. The capriciousness of Trump’s economic policies has also caused uncertainty: since Trump's inauguration, consumer sentiment has fallen, and more North Carolinians expect a recession in the near term.
